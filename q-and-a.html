Q&A Briefing · Internal preparation document

Anticipated Questions & Approved Answers

Prepared talking points for the Sanofi Türkiye spokesperson on the Plaquenil Regulated Access Framework. Answer in your own words; do not read verbatim.

Internal use — spokesperson briefing This document is prepared for use by the Sanofi Türkiye spokesperson during the launch press conference and follow-up media engagements. Each answer is followed by a bridge — the talking point you pivot to once you have addressed the question, so every answer ends back on a key message. Reserve answers at the bottom are used only if a question is pushed beyond the standard set.

Question 01 · expected from health journalists

Is this framework really a public-health measure, or is it designed to protect Sanofi's profit?

Answer. No. SGK reimburses Plaquenil at the State-negotiated regulated price, and the framework introduces no premium pricing whatsoever. What Sanofi gains is predictability — the ability to plan production against a verified, stable patient population rather than panic-driven demand spikes. The State, the patient, and Sanofi share the upside.

Bridge → "And critically, the registry is shared infrastructure: any generic manufacturer in Türkiye can dispense through the same verified channel. There is no extension of market exclusivity."

Question 02 · expected from political and policy press

What happens during the next pandemic, when there is again speculation about off-label use of this drug?

Answer. The framework's Crisis-Trigger clause is built exactly for that scenario. Off-label dispensing is automatically suspended the moment WHO declares a public-health emergency. It can be reactivated — but only after credible, peer-reviewed clinical evidence supports the new indication. The default is to protect chronic patients first, while science catches up.

Bridge → "Off-label use does not disappear — it simply moves through TİTCK's existing named-patient pathway, which already evaluates each case on its scientific merits. We are not closing a door; we are putting the right door in the right place."

Question 03 · expected from competition and industry analysts

Does this framework delay access for generic versions of hydroxychloroquine?

Answer. No. The Digital Health Pass is hosted by the Ministry of Health, not by Sanofi. It is shared infrastructure. Any generic manufacturer authorised in Türkiye dispenses through the same verified channel. The framework does not extend market exclusivity, does not change pricing, and does not advantage Sanofi commercially.

Bridge → "In fact, by stabilising the patient population through a verified registry, generic manufacturers gain better demand visibility — which historically supports lower-cost production planning."

Question 04 · expected from fiscal and budget reporters

Why should SGK pay to set this up?

Answer. Because the alternative is more expensive. Maintenance therapy for a stable lupus patient costs roughly $1,500 per year. A single severe lupus flare — a hospitalization or ICU admission — costs around $75,000. Preventing one flare funds maintenance therapy for fifty patients. The 12–18 month payback period on infrastructure is conservative; the State's downside risk is small relative to the cost of doing nothing.

Bridge → "These are not theoretical numbers. The 2020 crisis produced a measurable wave of preventable hospitalizations — the SGK absorbed the cost. The framework moves that money from emergency response into infrastructure that prevents the emergency in the first place."

Question 05 · expected from international and policy press

Could other countries adopt this model?

Answer. Yes — and we hope they will. After eighteen months of national operation in Türkiye, we plan to share the framework with the European Medicines Agency and MENA regional regulators as the «Plaquenil Model». Türkiye is the policy laboratory; the long-term ambition is global. The framework parallels FDA REMS programs, which now cover seventy-three active US products — but adapts to public health systems rather than private insurance.

Bridge → "Türkiye gets first-mover credit. The framework is intentionally exportable — it is built on internationally recognised principles, not Turkish exceptions."

Question 06 · expected from patient advocacy and civil society

How will patient privacy be protected in a national registry?

Answer. The registry is hosted by the Ministry of Health, not by Sanofi. Patient identifiers are accessible only to verified prescribers and pharmacists through e-Nabız credentials. Sanofi receives aggregate stock and dispensing data only — never patient-level information. The system is designed to comply with KVKK and GDPR from day one.

Bridge → "Patient privacy is a precondition of patient trust, and patient trust is a precondition of the framework working at all. We treat it that way."

Question 07 · expected from medical professional bodies

What if doctors disagree with the verification requirements?

Answer. The framework is being co-designed with the Turkish Rheumatology Society and consulted with the Türk Tabipleri Birliği. Physicians retain full prescribing authority — the registry verifies the diagnosis code, not the clinical decision. Off-label use remains possible for documented compassionate-use cases via TİTCK's existing per-patient approval pathway. This is exactly how the system works today; we are formalising the principle, not changing the practice.

Bridge → "Clinician autonomy and patient protection are not in tension here. The framework restricts off-label flow during emergencies, not clinical judgment for documented patients."

Reserve answers — use only if pushed

  • On compensation: "Sanofi is not seeking compensation for past shortages. The framework is forward-looking; the conversation is about the next decade, not the last one."
  • On other Sanofi products: "The model is portable but specific to chronic medicines with concentrated patient populations and clear diagnostic markers. Each candidate molecule would require its own regulatory impact assessment."
  • On timing: "Pilot launch in Q3 contingent on Ministry of Health approval; national rollout within nine months of that. We propose a fast, focused 90-day technical dialogue to begin."
  • On political risk: "We are presenting a public-health proposal, not a political one. The framework is designed to outlast any single administration."
  • On comparison to FDA REMS: "We are learning from REMS — particularly its ETASU patient-verification track record over fifteen-plus years — but adapting the model to a public health system. The principle travels; the execution is local."
