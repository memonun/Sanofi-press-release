Frequently Asked Questions · Regulated Access Framework

Common questions about the Regulated Access Framework

Answers to questions raised by journalists, patient organisations, policymakers and members of the public following the announcement.

Last updated — at the time of the launch press conference. Return to the full press release →

Is this framework really a public-health measure, or is it designed to protect Sanofi's profit?

It is a public-health measure. SGK reimburses Plaquenil at the State-negotiated regulated price, and the framework introduces no premium pricing whatsoever. The system is built to plan production against a verified, stable patient population — replacing the speculative spikes that caused the 2020 shortage. The State, the patient and Sanofi share the upside.

What happens during the next pandemic, when there is again speculation about off-label use of this drug?

The framework includes a Crisis-Trigger clause built exactly for that scenario. Off-label dispensing is automatically suspended the moment WHO declares a public-health emergency, and is reactivated only when peer-reviewed clinical evidence supports the new indication. Off-label use does not disappear — it moves through TİTCK's existing named-patient pathway, which evaluates each case on its scientific merits. The default is to protect chronic patients first, while science catches up.

Does this framework delay access for generic versions of hydroxychloroquine?

No. The Digital Health Pass is hosted by the Ministry of Health, not by Sanofi. It is shared infrastructure. Any generic manufacturer authorised in Türkiye dispenses through the same verified channel. The framework does not extend market exclusivity, does not change pricing, and does not give Sanofi a commercial advantage.

Why should SGK pay to set this up?

Because the alternative is more expensive. Maintenance therapy for a stable lupus patient costs roughly $1,500 per year. A single severe lupus flare — typically requiring hospitalisation or ICU admission — costs around $75,000. Preventing one flare therefore funds maintenance therapy for fifty patients. The infrastructure has a projected 12 to 18 month payback period. The 2020 crisis already produced a measurable wave of preventable hospitalisations; the framework moves that money from emergency response into prevention.

Could other countries adopt this model?

Yes, and the framework is designed for it. After eighteen months of national operation in Türkiye, Sanofi plans to share the model with the European Medicines Agency and regional regulators in the Middle East and North Africa as the «Plaquenil Model». The approach parallels established US precedent — FDA REMS programs now cover more than seventy active products with patient-verification controls — but adapts it to public health systems rather than private insurance.

How will patient privacy be protected in a national registry?

The registry is hosted by the Ministry of Health, not by Sanofi. Patient identifiers are accessible only to verified prescribers and pharmacists through e-Nabız credentials. Sanofi receives aggregate stock and dispensing data only — never patient-level information. The system is designed to comply with KVKK and GDPR requirements from day one.

What if doctors disagree with the verification requirements?

The framework is being co-designed with the Turkish Rheumatology Society and consulted with the Türk Tabipleri Birliği. Physicians retain full prescribing authority — the registry verifies the diagnosis code, not the clinical decision. Off-label use remains possible for documented compassionate-use cases via TİTCK's existing per-patient approval pathway. The framework formalises a principle, not the day-to-day practice.
